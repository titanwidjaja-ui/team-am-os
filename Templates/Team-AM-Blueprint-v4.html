Why sodium matters more than you think
Sodium drives fluid into your muscle cells, powers every muscle contraction through nerve impulse transmission, and maintains the blood volume that delivers nutrients to working muscles. Without enough sodium, you can drink all the water you want and still be functionally dehydrated at the cellular level. Low sodium directly reduces strength, explosiveness, endurance, and your ability to sustain work capacity across a full session.
On bloating
The water retention from adequate sodium is intramuscular. It's water being held inside your muscle cells where it belongs. This makes muscles look fuller, perform better, and recover faster. The puffy, soft bloat people worry about comes from inconsistent sodium intake. Consistent daily intake eliminates this within 1-2 weeks.
Why potassium matches sodium
Every cell runs on the sodium-potassium pump. Sodium pulls water to the outside of cells, potassium pulls it inside. You need both in balance for true cellular hydration. High sodium without potassium means water sits outside your muscle cells, which is the kind of bloat nobody wants. Potassium also regulates blood pressure, prevents cramping, and supports heart rhythm. As your sodium goes up, potassium must follow.
How carbs affect your water
Every gram of stored glycogen holds about 3g of water. When you start eating in a deficit, glycogen drops and you lose water weight fast. That initial drop is fluid, not fat. After that, expect a slower and steadier decline.
How alcohol affects your hydration
Alcohol is a diuretic. It increases fluid loss and disrupts sleep architecture even when you feel fine. 3-4 drinks on a weekend night can set your recovery back 1-2 days. Not forbidden, but the cost is real.
How to adjust based on symptoms
Feeling puffy
Check sodium consistency and potassium balance. Puffiness usually means sodium is inconsistent or potassium is too low. Stabilize both daily amounts.
Feeling flat
Check carb intake and glycogen. Likely under-fueled. May need a refeed or carb bump on training days.
Cramping
Usually a sodium-potassium imbalance, not just dehydration. Increase both together and reassess in 48 hours. Pre-load sodium before your next session.
Why this hydration setup fits you
You're at about 64 oz a day right now, mostly around training. Bumping to 130 oz spread throughout the day will improve your digestion, reduce the bloating, and support the deficit phase. The biggest change is drinking consistently all day instead of loading up around workouts.
