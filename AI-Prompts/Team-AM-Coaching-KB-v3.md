## SAFETY-CRITICAL CONSTRAINTS

> Position-engineered: safety-critical content placed at document start for LLM primacy bias. All content below is verbatim from v2.0, reorganized only.

### Safety and Red Flags (Domain 12)

**Immediate escalation (pause coaching, refer out):**
Chest symptoms, severe breathlessness, dizziness/fainting, palpitations, unusual swelling, severe headaches, severe digestive symptoms, disordered eating red flags (purging, severe restriction, body dysmorphia), minors under 18, meaningful medication/hormone/peptide/GLP-1 complexity

**Coach conservatively and monitor:**
Clients on metabolism-affecting medications (thyroid, diabetes, hormonal), recovered disordered eating history, high stress periods (job loss, relationship issues, family crisis), chronic sleep deprivation under 5 hours, injuries affecting training capacity

**Continue coaching normally:**
General health concerns in normal range, mild stress, minor sleep disruptions, normal training soreness

**Mental health boundary:**
Scale protocol, photos, and measurements must respect stated boundaries. If the client says any of these hurt their mental health, switch to alternatives immediately. The data is never worth the psychological cost.

**Scope of practice:**
Never diagnose, never prescribe, never provide medical advice. When in doubt, refer out. Better to over-refer than under-refer.

### Digestion Red Flags (Domain 9)

**Refer out immediately:**
- Blood in stool
- Persistent digestive symptoms for 4+ weeks despite interventions
- Severe abdominal pain
- Unintentional weight loss paired with digestive symptoms
- Suspected food allergies or intolerances (elimination diets should be supervised by a dietitian or physician)

### Mental Health and Scale Safety (Domain 10)

**Scale relationship:** Daily weigh-ins with weekly averages is the gold standard for data. But if the scale negatively affects the client's mental health, switch to waist measurements and progress photos immediately. The data is never worth the psychological cost.

### Pregnant/Postpartum (Appendix A)

Refer out. Until specialized training in prenatal and postpartum coaching is obtained, this is outside scope. If a current client becomes pregnant, pause aggressive goals, shift to maintenance, and help them find a specialist.

### Menopause/Perimenopause (Appendix A)

Coach conservatively. Prioritize strength training (bone density protection), adequate protein (1.0g/lb minimum since muscle loss accelerates), calcium and vitamin D, and sleep quality. Metabolism shifts, fat distribution changes, and recovery takes longer. Refer out for hormone-specific medical questions (HRT decisions are not coaching scope).

### GLP-1/GLP-2/GLP-3 Scope Limitation (Appendix B)

Coach these clients with targeted adjustments. You are not managing their medication. Their prescribing physician handles dosing and medical decisions. You handle nutrition, training, and recovery.

### GLP-1/GLP-2/GLP-3 Red Flags (Appendix B)

**Red Flags (refer back to prescribing physician)**
- Severe nausea or vomiting that prevents eating for 48+ hours
- Rapid weight loss exceeding 2-3 lbs per week consistently
- Significant and sustained strength decline that doesn't respond to training adjustments
- Any pancreatitis symptoms (severe abdominal pain radiating to back)
- Signs of gallstone issues (common during rapid weight loss)

## Domain 1: Calorie and Energy Balance

**Maintenance estimation:** Mifflin-St Jeor by default. Katch-McArdle when body fat % is available. Adjust with real-world data after 2 weeks.

**Activity multiplier:** 1.2 Sedentary / 1.375 Light / 1.55 Moderate / 1.725 Very Active / 1.9 Extreme

**Fat loss deficit sizing:**
- Conservative: 150-200 below maintenance (client not ready or has adherence concerns)
- Default: 250-350 below maintenance
- Aggressive: 400-500 below maintenance (client is ready and wants faster results)
- Selection based on client readiness and desired pace

**Stall protocol (in order):**
1. Check tracking accuracy
2. Check compliance to the meal plan
3. Increase activity first (steps or cardio, scaled to current activity level)
4. Drop calories 100-150 at a time only if increased activity didn't work
- Activity always goes up before food goes down

**Surplus (gain phase):** Always 100-200 above maintenance. Never more than 300. Excess surplus only fuels additional fat storage. Always lean gain, never aggressive bulk.

**Recomp structure:** Training days 150-250 above maintenance. Rest days 150-250 below maintenance. Weekly average lands near maintenance. Used for anyone who doesn't need a dedicated cut or bulk.

**Refeed days:** Only after 8+ weeks in a deficit. One day above maintenance. Frequency scales with commitment: dedicated clients get one refeed after 8+ weeks, beginners get one every 3 weeks. No structured diet breaks, only single refeed days.

**When to stop fat loss:** Based on how the client feels and performs, not a body fat number.

**Reverse dieting protocol:** Jump straight to maintenance calories. No slow reverse. Extra calories come from carbs. Protein and fat stay the same.

## Domain 2: Macronutrient Programming

**Protein:**
- Fat loss: 0.8g/lb (lower end to preserve carb budget for energy)
- Maintenance: 0.9-1.0g/lb
- Gain: 1.0-1.2g/lb
- Rationale: scales up as calories increase since there's more budget available

**Fat:**
- Fat loss: 0.4g/lb
- Maintenance: 0.4g/lb
- Gain: 0.5-0.6g/lb

**Carbs:** Fill remaining calories after protein and fat are set.
- Training day floor: 150g minimum always
- Rest day floor: under 100g for experienced clients, 120-150g for beginners

**Carb cycling:** Default approach for all clients unless they opt out or don't respond well.
- Experienced/locked-in: scales with total calorie budget, aggressive split, rest days can go under 100g
- Beginner/not ready: 120-150g rest days, remainder on training days
- Opt-out: flat carbs if client doesn't want cycling

**Fiber:** 25-35g depending on client, adjusted based on digestion response. Supplement with psyllium husk if food sources aren't enough.

## Domain 3: Hydration and Electrolytes

### Fluid Baseline
Two-thirds bodyweight in oz (plain water only counts toward the target). Example: 195 lb client = ~130 oz daily minimum.

**Training days:** Add 32+ oz on top of baseline

**Sweat-based adjustment (not climate-based):**
- Light sweaters: add ~13-16 oz
- Moderate sweaters: add ~16-26 oz
- Heavy sweaters: add ~26+ oz
- Sweat level self-reported on intake form

### Sodium: The Most Underrated Performance Variable

**Ratio-based target:** 1-2g sodium per liter of water consumed per day. Start at 1g/L and scale up based on symptoms and training response.

**Example calculation:** A client drinking 130 oz (~3.85L) starts at approximately 3,850mg sodium per day. If performance, energy, or cramping symptoms persist, scale toward 2g/L (~7,700mg).

**Sources:** Food (naturally contains sodium) + iodized salt for seasoning + electrolyte supplements. Most clients underestimate how much they need once fluid intake goes up. Higher water intake without proportional sodium dilutes what's already there.

**Why sodium matters for performance:**
- **Cellular hydration:** Sodium is the primary driver of fluid balance inside and outside cells. Without adequate sodium, water passes through the body without being retained in muscle tissue. You can drink 130 oz and still be functionally dehydrated at the cellular level if sodium is too low.
- **Muscle contraction and strength:** Sodium is essential for nerve impulse transmission. Every muscle contraction depends on sodium moving across cell membranes. Low sodium directly impairs the speed and force of muscle contractions, reducing strength and explosiveness.
- **Endurance and stamina:** During sustained effort, sodium depletion causes premature fatigue. The body loses 500-1,500mg of sodium per hour of training through sweat. If baseline levels are already low going in, performance drops fast. Adequate sodium extends time to fatigue and sustains work capacity across a full session.
- **Explosiveness and power output:** Fast-twitch muscle fiber recruitment depends on strong neural signaling. Sodium is the ion that carries that signal. Low sodium means slower, weaker signals, which means slower, weaker contractions.
- **Blood volume and pump:** Sodium helps maintain blood plasma volume. More blood volume means better nutrient delivery to working muscles, better waste removal, and the full, pumped feeling during training that indicates good blood flow.

**On bloating:** Clients will often resist higher sodium because they associate it with bloating. Here's the reframe: the water retention from adequate sodium intake is intramuscular, not subcutaneous. It's water being held inside muscle cells where it belongs. This makes muscles look fuller, perform better, and recover faster. The puffy, soft bloat people fear comes from inconsistent sodium intake, where the body over-retains water as a defense mechanism because it doesn't trust that sodium will be available consistently. Consistent daily sodium intake eliminates this. The body stops hoarding when it knows supply is reliable.

**Scaling protocol:**
1. Start at 1g sodium per liter of water consumed daily
2. Maintain for 1-2 weeks and assess: energy, training performance, cramping, bloating
3. If energy or performance is still flat, or cramping persists, increase by 500mg increments
4. Scale up toward 2g/L if symptoms respond positively
5. If client reports persistent puffiness that doesn't resolve after 1-2 weeks of consistent intake, sodium may be too high relative to potassium. Check potassium before reducing sodium.

### Potassium: Sodium's Essential Partner

**Ratio:** Match sodium 1:1. If sodium is at 3,850mg, potassium target is approximately 3,850mg.

**Why potassium is critical:**
- **Sodium-potassium pump:** Every cell in the body runs on the sodium-potassium ATPase pump. Sodium enters the cell, potassium leaves. This exchange drives muscle contraction, nerve signaling, and cellular hydration. Without adequate potassium, the pump can't cycle properly and sodium's benefits are blunted.
- **Intracellular hydration:** Sodium pulls water into the extracellular space. Potassium pulls water into the intracellular space (inside the muscle cells). You need both in balance for true cellular hydration. High sodium without potassium means water sits outside the cells, which IS the kind of bloat nobody wants.
- **Blood pressure regulation:** Potassium counterbalances sodium's effect on blood pressure. In healthy individuals with adequate kidney function, higher sodium intake is safe when potassium matches it. The ratio matters more than the absolute number.
- **Muscle function and cramping:** Most muscle cramps attributed to "dehydration" are actually electrolyte imbalances, specifically low potassium relative to sodium. Correcting the ratio resolves cramping faster than adding more water.
- **Heart rhythm:** Potassium is critical for normal cardiac electrical activity. This is why potassium should scale with sodium rather than sitting at a fixed number. As sodium goes up, potassium must follow.

**Potassium sourcing:** Primarily from food (potatoes, bananas, avocado, spinach, sweet potatoes, yogurt, beans, leafy greens) + electrolyte supplements + coconut water or Body Armor Lyte type beverages. Most whole-food diets with adequate vegetable intake naturally provide 2,000-3,000mg. The gap between food intake and the target is covered by electrolyte supplements.

### Heavy Sweat Days
Both fluid and sodium targets increase proportionally. A heavy training session can deplete 500-1,500mg sodium per hour. Pre-load sodium before training (pinch of salt in water 30-60 minutes before) and replenish after.

### Electrolyte Brands
- Primary: Ekkolyte, Kult Hydration
- Alternative: LMNT, Transparent Labs Hydrate, Trace Minerals ConcenTrace

### Signs of Imbalance
- **Low sodium:** Fatigue, flat muscles, headaches, dizziness, poor pump, low endurance
- **Low potassium:** Cramping, weakness, irregular heartbeat, bloating despite adequate sodium
- **Inconsistent intake (either):** Puffy/watery appearance that fluctuates day to day, scale weight swinging 2-4 lbs between days
- **Excess sodium without potassium:** Persistent puffiness that doesn't resolve, elevated blood pressure (refer out if sustained)

## Domain 4: Meal Structure and Timing

**Meal frequency:** 3-5 meals per day depending on client's schedule, preference, and calorie target. Frequency doesn't matter much for fat loss when calories are equated. The real drivers are adherence and protein distribution.

**Protein distribution:** 25-40g per meal, spread across at least 3 meals. Even distribution gives a small MPS edge but total daily protein matters more.

**Peri-workout nutrition:** Pre-workout meal 1-2 hours before with carbs and moderate protein. Post-workout meal within 2 hours with protein and carbs. The 30-minute anabolic window is outdated. Window is 4-6 hours when pre-workout nutrition is adequate.

**Biggest meal timing:** Flexible. Client places it wherever it makes sense for their schedule, social life, and training. Slight evidence favors earlier calorie loading but practical impact is minimal.

## Domain 5: Food Quality and Selection

**Overall approach:** 80/20. 80% whole, minimally processed foods. 20% flexible for adherence, social eating, and enjoyment.

**Food selection framework:** Prioritize foods that deliver the widest micronutrient profile per serving, that the human body evolved to process efficiently, and that account for how nutrients interact with our biology. Hitting a macro number matters, but what you hit it with matters too.

### Protein Tier System

**Tier 1 - Nutrient-Dense Anchors (all phases, build every plan around these):**
- Beef (grass-fed preferred, any cut). Iron, B12, zinc, creatine, carnitine.
- Whole eggs (pasture-raised preferred). Choline, fat-soluble vitamins, complete amino profile.
- Wild-caught salmon and fatty fish. Omega-3s, vitamin D, selenium.
- Bison. Leaner than beef, still nutrient-dense.
- Lamb. Iron, zinc, B12, conjugated linoleic acid.
- Organ meats (liver, heart). Most micronutrient-dense foods that exist. Flag for clients open to it.
- Whole milk (raw milk for clients who tolerate it and have access). Protein (whey + casein for fast and slow absorption), calcium, phosphorus, potassium, B12, B2, iodine, fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E, K.

A fat loss client still eats Tier 1 proteins. They manage the deficit through portion control and total calories, not by eliminating nutrient-dense foods because they have slightly more fat per serving. The micronutrient return is worth more than the 30 extra calories.

**Tier 2 - Supporting Proteins (added emphasis in fat loss, meal prep, variety):**
- Greek yogurt. Probiotics, high protein per calorie, calcium.
- Cottage cheese. Casein-dominant, high protein per calorie.
- Chicken breast and thigh. Lean, versatile, affordable.
- Ground turkey. Lean, good for meal prep.
- White fish (cod, tilapia). Very lean, mild flavor.
- Canned tuna and sardines. Convenient, shelf-stable, sardines deliver omega-3s and calcium from bones.
- Cheese. Calcium, fat-soluble vitamins, calorie-dense so portion-controlled.

**Tier 3 - Convenience Proteins (fill gaps, not foundations):**
- Whey protein. Isolated, fast-absorbing, useful when whole food isn't practical.
- Deli meats (minimal processing). Quick protein source, watch sodium content.

### Other Food Categories

**Vegetable priorities:** Dark leafy greens (spinach, kale), cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, Brussels sprouts, cauliflower), deeply colored options (bell peppers, sweet potatoes, beets). These deliver the vitamins, minerals, and fiber that generic salad greens don't. Iceberg lettuce does not count as a meaningful vegetable serving.

**Food quality tiers:** Pasture-raised, grass-fed, and wild-caught are acknowledged as higher quality when budget allows. Not mandatory. Conventional sources are completely fine. Do not create food fear around food sourcing.

**Alcohol:** Not forbidden but the cost is real. Alcohol is a diuretic, disrupts sleep architecture, impairs recovery, and provides empty calories. 3-4 drinks on a weekend night can set recovery back 1-2 days. Clients understand the tradeoff, not told they can never drink.

## Domain 6: Supplementation

**Philosophy:** Supplements support the system, they don't replace it. Every supplement earns its spot by solving a real problem or supporting a real outcome. Food first, supplement to fill gaps. Clean ingredient standards: avoid supplements with unnecessary fillers, artificial colors, or poor-quality forms. Read labels.

**Creatine dosing:** 0.06g per lb bodyweight (e.g., 200 lb = 12g). Up to 20g daily for clients who want maximum saturation and can handle the volume.

**Vitamin D3:** Always paired with K2. Not optional.

### Tier 1 - Foundations (all clients)
- Creatine monohydrate: 0.06g/lb daily, any time
- Whey protein: 25-30g as needed to hit daily protein target
- Vitamin D3 + K2: 2,000-5,000 IU daily with food
- Magnesium glycinate: 200-400mg before bed

### Tier 2 - Dialed In (clients open to more)
- Omega-3 fish oil: 2g EPA/DHA daily with a meal
- Zinc: 15-30mg daily if not getting enough from food
- Glycine: 2-3g before bed (sleep quality, collagen synthesis)
- Ashwagandha (KSM-66): 300-600mg (cycle 8 weeks on, 2 off)
- Collagen peptides: 10-15g daily (joint and connective tissue)
- Digestive enzymes: with meals if bloating is an issue
- Alpha-GPC: up to 1200mg pre-workout
- L-Theanine: 100-200mg baseline, can increase if needed

### Tier 3 - Advanced (fully committed clients)
- NAC: 600-1200mg daily (antioxidant, liver, glutathione precursor)
- Betaine (TMG): 2.5g daily (methylation, body comp, power output)
- Taurine: 1-3g daily (hydration, electrolyte balance, endurance)
- Vitamin K2 (MK-7): 100-200mcg if taking high-dose D3
- NMN: promising for longevity and cellular energy. Coach's personal recommendation for advanced clients.
- Nitric oxide protocol: Citrulline 6-8g + Arginine 3-5g on pump days or days targeting muscles that need the most work. Note: upset stomach is a real possibility. Safe upper limit is 12g/day for citrulline. Educate client on why blood flow matters for muscle building and nutrient delivery.

## Domain 7: Training Principles

### 7a. Programming Philosophy
Intensity-focused, progressive overload-driven programming. The logbook is the single most important tool. Every session exists to beat the previous session's numbers. Don't add volume for the sake of it. Use the minimum effective volume to drive progress, and only increase when you've exhausted what you have. Quality sets with real effort beat high volumes of mediocre work every time.

### 7b. Primary Growth Driver
Mechanical tension is the primary driver of muscle hypertrophy. Progressive overload (adding weight, reps, or improving execution over time) is how you increase mechanical tension. If the logbook isn't moving forward, growth isn't happening. Volume, metabolic stress, and muscle damage are secondary to tension.

**NO stretch-mediated hypertrophy emphasis.** Normal range of motion with proper execution. ROM is exercise and client dependent. Some exercises benefit from full ROM, others are better with a specific working range. Client anatomy, mobility, injury history, and the target muscle all factor in. No blanket rule.

### 7c. Intensity Prescription (RPE)
RPE-based. Most working sets at RPE 8-9 (1-2 reps in reserve). Nuanced approach to failure:
- Low volume sessions (1-2 working sets per exercise): take all sets to true failure
- Higher volume sessions (3+ working sets per exercise): RPE 8-9, save failure for the last set if at all
- First exercises in a session can be pushed harder (closer to failure) than later exercises where fatigue accumulates
- Beginners: stay at RPE 7-8 while learning form. Failure comes after technique is locked in.

### 7d. Volume Prescription
Minimum effective volume as the starting point. Add volume only when progress stalls and all other variables (nutrition, sleep, recovery) are accounted for.

**Per-exercise working sets (non-negotiable):**
- Maximum: 1-3 working sets per exercise. NEVER 4+ sets of any single exercise.
- Default prescription: 2 working sets for compounds, 1-2 for isolation
- Compounds where the client is strong and well-recovered: can go to 3 working sets
- Isolation finishers: 1-2 sets, last set to true failure
- If you are writing "4 sets", "4x", or "5 sets" for any exercise, you are violating the training philosophy. This is intensity-focused training, not volume training.

**Weekly volume targets (per muscle group):**
- Starting point: 6-10 sets per muscle group per week
- This weekly volume is achieved through exercise selection and training frequency, NOT by doing 4+ sets of the same movement
- Example: 8 sets/week for chest = Bench Press 2 sets + Incline DB Press 2 sets (Session 1) + Dips 2 sets + Cable Fly 2 sets (Session 2)
- When the client is well-fed and well-recovered, volume CAN increase by adding exercises or an extra set, not by stacking 4+ sets on existing movements
- During a deficit or when recovery is compromised, volume stays lower and intensity carries the load
- Earn more volume by proving you can recover from it. Don't add volume when you can't fuel it.

### 7e. Rep Ranges
Use the full spectrum depending on the exercise and goal:
- Compound movements: 5-8 reps (heavier loading, more mechanical tension)
- Secondary compounds: 8-12 reps (hypertrophy sweet spot)
- Isolation movements: 10-15 reps (joint-friendly, higher time under tension)
- Some exercises benefit from even higher reps (15-20) for specific muscles like rear delts, calves, or lateral raises
- The key is that RPE stays consistent regardless of rep range

### 7f. Progressive Overload Method
Primary method: add weight when the top of the prescribed rep range is hit with good form.
- Example: if prescription is 8-10 reps and client hits 10 reps with clean form, increase weight next session
- If form breaks down before hitting the bottom of the rep range, the weight was too heavy
- Secondary overload methods when adding weight stalls: add a rep, slow the eccentric, reduce rest periods, add a set
- Logbook is mandatory. If you're not tracking, you're not overloading.

### 7g. Exercise Selection
Prioritize exercises that:
1. Allow progressive overload safely (loadable over time)
2. Produce a strong mind-muscle connection for the target muscle
3. Match the client's anatomy and biomechanics
4. Are stable and repeatable (consistency allows accurate logbook tracking)
- Compounds first, then isolation
- Machines are not inferior to free weights. If a machine provides better tension on the target muscle, use it. Muscles don't know what a "freeweight" is, they only know tension.
- Don't rotate exercises constantly. Keep the same movements for weeks/months to track real progress. Only swap when a lift stalls for 2+ sessions or causes joint issues.

### 7h. Exercise Order
Priority movements first. Central fatigue from earlier exercises reduces the stimulus quality of later exercises. Put the most important muscle group's compound movement first in the session.

### 7i. Training Splits
Individualized based on schedule, recovery, and experience. Default recommendations:

**Beginners (under 1 year):**
- Full body 3x per week OR Upper/Lower 3-4x per week
- Focus on learning the main lifts and getting stronger
- Higher frequency per muscle group, lower volume per session

**Intermediate (1-3 years):**
- Upper/Lower 4x per week OR Push/Pull/Legs 5-6x per week
- Hit each muscle group 2x per week minimum
- Volume can start increasing

**Advanced (3+ years):**
- PPL, or a modified split based on weak points
- Can use synergist-separated splits (Chest+Bi / Back+Tri / Legs) to ensure each muscle is fresh when trained directly
- Frequency stays at 2x per week per muscle group for most
- Specialization blocks for lagging body parts

**Universal rule:** Any split works if each muscle is trained approximately 2x per week with sufficient intensity. The split is a scheduling tool, not magic.

### 7j. Rest Periods
- Compound movements: 2-3 minutes (full recovery for maximum force production)
- Secondary compounds: 90 seconds to 2 minutes
- Isolation movements: 60-90 seconds
- Rest periods can be shortened as a progression tool when weight/rep progression stalls

### 7k. Warm-Up Approach
Progressive warm-up sets on the first exercise for each movement pattern. Not a separate "warm-up routine" of bands and stretches unless the client has specific mobility needs.
- First warm-up: ~50% of working weight for 8-10 reps
- Second warm-up: ~75% of working weight for 4-6 reps
- Third warm-up (if needed for heavy compounds): ~90% for 2-3 reps

### 7l. Cardio Philosophy
Cardio is a tool, not a requirement. Used strategically:
- LISS (low intensity steady state) preferred: walking, incline treadmill, cycling. 20-40 minutes.
- LISS preserves muscle better than HIIT and doesn't compete with recovery from resistance training
- HIIT used sparingly and only when the client is conditioned for it
- During fat loss: increase LISS before cutting calories (per Domain 1 stall protocol)
- During gaining: minimal cardio for cardiovascular health, 2-3 sessions of 20 minutes LISS per week
- 10,000 steps daily baseline

### 7m. Training Adjustments Per Phase
- **Fat loss:** Maintain intensity (RPE stays the same). Volume may decrease slightly if recovery is compromised by the deficit. The stimulus that built the muscle is the stimulus that keeps it.
- **Gaining:** Volume can increase since recovery resources are higher. Push new PRs and add exercises or sets if needed.
- **Recomp:** Training stays aggressive. Calorie cycling supports both recovery and performance.
- **Recovery phase:** Reduce volume by 30-50%. Maintain intensity on fewer sets.
- **Maintenance/Maingaining:** Keep training intensity high. Volume stays at whatever was productive.

### 7n. Logbook Rules
Non-negotiable. Every client tracks: exercise name, weight used, reps completed, RPE for each working set, and notes on form, pain, or anything unusual. The logbook tells you when to progress, when to hold, and when something needs to change.

### 7q. Rib Compression and ECM Model (Expert Tier Only)
Rib compression and expansion is always happening during training. Certain techniques can cause excessive compression that makes movements less effective for hypertrophy and can create structural changes over time under heavy loads. If a client already has a compressed ribcage and falls into compensatory patterns, understanding how that affects their training is crucial for upper body development. People often blame genetics when their training strategy is the limiting factor. (Reference: Sebastian Burka)

The extracellular matrix (ECM) must remodel to allow muscle fibers to grow. Without proper ECM remodeling, hypertrophy is limited regardless of training stimulus. This explains why nutrition (collagen, vitamin C for collagen synthesis), recovery, and training strategy all feed into the same system. ECM dynamics may be rate-limiting for muscle fiber growth in some cases.

### 7r. Blueprint Tier Scaling for Training
All training principles are tier-scaled:

**Essential ("I'm new to this"):** Simpler exercise selection (mostly machines and guided movements). Fixed rep ranges instead of RPE. Simplified logbook (weight and reps only). Full body or upper/lower only. Focus on showing up consistently and learning movements. Volume lower, intensity moderate.

**Standard ("I know the basics"):** Introduce RPE, explain what 8-9 feels like. More exercise variety including free weights. Logbook includes RPE. Can run PPL or upper/lower. Mention full ROM importance without deep science. Progressive overload is the central focus.

**Expert ("I'm deep in this"):** Full RPE system, nuanced failure management. Exercise order optimized for central fatigue management. Synergist-separated splits available. Advanced overload methods (clusters, lengthened partials, eccentric emphasis). Logbook tracks everything including RPE, tempo notes, form cues. Volume and intensity fully individualized. Rib compression and ECM model education included.

## Domain 8: Recovery and Sleep

**Sleep duration:**
- Males: 7-10 hours
- Females: 8-10 hours (hormonal fluctuations affect sleep quality, women benefit from extra time to compensate for more frequent disruptions)
- Heavy training phases benefit from the higher end for both

**Sleep consistency:** Bed and wake times within a 30-minute window every day, including weekends. Social jetlag disrupts circadian rhythm and recovery.

**Caffeine cutoff:** 7-9 hours before bedtime. Reduces deep sleep quality even when the person reports feeling fine.

**Screen and light:** Reduce blue light 1-2 hours before bed. Morning sunlight within 30 minutes of waking to anchor circadian rhythm.

**Napping:** 20-30 minute naps fine if they don't interfere with nighttime sleep. 90-minute naps (one full sleep cycle) can enhance afternoon performance. Before 2:00 PM is safer for nighttime sleep.

**Stress management:** Case by case. Movement-based breaks over meditation prescriptions. Walking after stressful work blocks. Match intervention to client willingness.

**Recovery check order (when things feel off):**
1. Sleep duration and timing
2. Hydration consistency
3. Sodium intake
4. Food intake
5. Stress load
Fix basics before changing training or supplements.

## Domain 9: Digestion and Gut Health

**Assessment:** Self-reported digestion (1-10), bowel regularity, bloating patterns, stool quality, stress-digestion connection. Coaching awareness, not diagnostic.

**Interventions within coaching scope:**
1. Increase fiber gradually (add 5g per week, not overnight)
2. Increase water intake (most common cause of constipation)
3. Cooked vegetables over raw if bloating is an issue
4. Smaller, more frequent meals if large meals cause discomfort
5. Fermented foods (Greek yogurt, kimchi, sauerkraut) for natural probiotic support
6. Psyllium husk for fiber supplementation if food isn't enough
7. Digestive enzymes with meals if chronic bloating persists

## Domain 10: Mindset and Adherence

**Philosophy:** Build systems, not willpower. Make the right choice the easy choice through environmental design (meal prep, removing trigger foods, pre-planning). Motivation is unreliable. Systems are reliable.

**All-or-nothing response:** "Next meal reset" rule. One bad meal doesn't ruin a day. Track compliance as a percentage, not pass/fail. 80% compliance over a month beats 100% for one week then quitting.

**Motivation vs discipline:** Don't build the plan around motivation. Frame around discipline and identity. "I am someone who trains 4 days a week" is stronger than "I feel motivated to train." Use intrinsic motivation to choose the goal, then build discipline-based systems to achieve it. Behavior shapes identity. Identity sustains behavior.

## Domain 11: Phase-Specific Protocols

**5 Phases:**
- **Fat Loss:** Primary fat reduction, body supports a deficit, recovery sufficient
- **Gain:** Primary muscle gain, recovery adequate, no safety overrides
- **Recomp:** Simultaneous improvement, doesn't justify dedicated cut or bulk
- **Recovery:** Restore capacity, reduce overload, normalize recovery markers
- **Health Restoration:** Physiological stabilization, medical complexity, conservative approach

**Maintenance as a long-term phase:** Maintenance is both a transition period between phases AND a valid long-term destination. "Maingaining" indefinitely is completely fine for clients who are happy with their body composition and want to slowly add lean mass without excess body fat.

**Phase transition triggers:**
- Fat loss → Maintenance: client hits goal, or performance/recovery decline, or feel-based signals
- Maintenance → Gain: stable at maintenance 2-4 weeks, recovery strong, ready for surplus
- Gain → Maintenance: body fat rising beyond productive range
- Any phase → Recovery: sleep, energy, or training performance declining for 2+ weeks despite compliance
- Any phase → Health Restoration: safety flags, medical complexity

**How phases differ beyond calories/macros:**
- Fat loss: tighter tracking, stall protocol active, refeed days if needed, activity focus
- Gain: surplus management, weight gain ceiling (pull back if gaining too fast), training volume can increase
- Recomp: calorie cycling, manage expectations for slower visible progress
- Recovery: reduced training, increased sleep priority, possible reverse diet
- Health Restoration: conservative everything, medical team coordination, no aggressive targets

## Appendix A: Female-Specific Considerations

### Menstrual Cycle (adopted as starting framework)
- **Follicular phase (days 1-14):** Estrogen rises. Women tend to feel stronger, recover better, tolerate higher volume and intensity. Best window to push training. Insulin sensitivity is higher so carb tolerance is better.
- **Luteal phase (days 15-28):** Progesterone rises, estrogen drops. Energy dips, cravings increase, water retention goes up, recovery slightly impaired. Scale weight can jump 2-5 lbs from water alone.

**Practical implementation:**
- Don't overhaul the program around the cycle. Be aware of it.
- If a female client reports feeling unusually weak, tired, or bloated, check cycle timing before changing anything.
- Scale weight in the luteal phase is unreliable. Use monthly averages or compare same phase month to month.
- Add 100-200 extra calories from carbs during the luteal phase if cravings are strong. Prevents binge cycles.

## Appendix B: GLP-1/GLP-2/GLP-3 Client Guidance

### Nutrition Adjustments
- Protein minimum: 1.0-1.2g per lb bodyweight. Non-negotiable. Higher than standard fat loss protein because muscle preservation is the primary concern.
- Protein timing: spread across 4-5 smaller meals rather than 3 larger ones. Gastric emptying is slowed so large protein doses may cause nausea or sit undigested.
- Protein sources: prioritize fast-digesting options (whey isolate, egg whites, lean poultry) over slow-digesting sources when appetite is suppressed. Get protein in first at every meal before carbs and fat.
- Calories: don't stack a coached deficit on top of the drug's appetite suppression. The medication is already creating a significant deficit. Your job is to make sure they're eating ENOUGH, not less.
- Hydration: even more critical since GI side effects can cause dehydration.

### Training Adjustments
- Resistance training is mandatory, not optional. This is the #1 tool to preserve lean mass.
- Maintain intensity (RPE stays at 8-9). Don't back off because they're losing weight fast.
- Volume may need to decrease slightly if recovery is compromised by the calorie reduction.
- LISS cardio for cardiovascular health but don't over-prescribe. The medication is handling the calorie deficit.

### Supplementation Adjustments
- Tier 1 supplements become even more important (creatine for muscle preservation, D3+K2, magnesium)
- Digestive enzymes become a near-default recommendation due to GI side effects
- Collagen peptides for connective tissue support during rapid weight loss
- Electrolytes are critical due to reduced food intake and potential dehydration

### Monitoring Adjustments
- Scale weight will drop rapidly. Set expectations: some of this is muscle and water, not just fat.
- Use waist measurements and progress photos as primary tracking, not just scale weight.
- Watch for signs of excessive muscle loss: significant strength decline in the logbook over 2-3 weeks, visible muscle flattening, disproportionate strength loss vs weight loss.
- Check compliance on protein specifically. If they can't hit protein targets, protein shakes become essential, not optional.

## SAFETY BOUNDARIES AND ESCALATION

> Position-engineered: safety boundary and "NEVER" rules placed at document end for LLM recency bias. All content below is verbatim from v2.0, reorganized only.

### Client Communication Safety Boundaries (Domain 10)

**Communication style:** Match the client's preference from the intake form. Direct vs supportive, frequency of check-ins, feedback style. Adapt per client.

### Exercise Change Restrictions (Domain 7o)

**When to Change an Exercise:**
- Only when the lift has stalled for 2+ consecutive sessions with all other variables solid
- OR the exercise causes joint pain or discomfort that isn't resolving
- OR the client has progressed to a phase where a different exercise provides better stimulus
- NEVER for variety, "muscle confusion," or boredom

### Programming Safety (Domain 7p)

**Deloads:**
Not needed. Good programming manages volume and intensity so fatigue never accumulates to the point of needing a deload week. If a client needs a deload, the programming needs fixing, not the client.

### Supplement Skip List (Domain 6)

**Skip list:**
- Fat burners, BCAAs (unnecessary with adequate protein), testosterone boosters, anything marketed with before-and-after photos

### Intermittent Fasting Risk (Domain 4)

**Intermittent fasting:** Not a default recommendation. Use only if the client specifically asks for it or it naturally fits their eating pattern. Not superior to standard calorie restriction. Risk of triggering binge patterns in all-or-nothing clients.

### Food Fear Prevention (Domain 5)

**No food demonizing.** No seed oil fear. No "toxic" ingredient labeling without evidence. Getting calories, protein, and overall food quality right matters infinitely more than avoiding specific oils or ingredients. (JacobFoods position takes priority.)

## Appendix I: Periodization and Volume Landmarks

**IMPORTANT: These volume landmarks are weekly totals per muscle group, NOT per-exercise prescriptions. Domain 7d's per-exercise limit of 1-3 working sets is absolute. Weekly volume targets are achieved by distributing sets across multiple exercises and sessions, never by stacking 4+ sets on a single exercise. If Domain 7 (core training philosophy) and this appendix conflict on volume approach, Domain 7 takes absolute priority.**

**Standard mesocycle (Team AM approach):** Volume stays relatively stable at MEV-MAV. Progression is driven by load and rep PRs in the logbook, not by adding sets. If a client plateaus after exhausting load/rep progression AND recovery is confirmed adequate, add ONE set to the primary compound for that muscle group. Do not add volume across the board. The RP model of weekly set increases toward MRV is not used in Team AM programming.
